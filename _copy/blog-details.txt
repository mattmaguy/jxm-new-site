[H2]  [3w]
  Sonar? So What.

[DEK]  [9w]
  Attention is the ocean. Intention is the ping back.

[BODY]  [11w]
  Level 0x13 — Ecco the Dolphin · Sega Genesis · 1992

[BODY]  [82w]
  You remember the moment. Saturday morning, controller in hand, watching a dolphin breach into a
  perfect blue sky, and then the entire pod vanishes. Sucked into a vortex. Gone. Just Ecco, alone
  in a suddenly very hostile ocean. You swam toward the first thing that moved. It ate you. You
  tried again. Drowned. Again. Got eaten again. The ocean in Ecco the Dolphin is enormous, and it is
  patient, and it will absolutely outlast you if you keep chasing everything in it.

[H3]  [5w]
  Just Press A and Listen.

[BODY]  [65w]
  At some point, you figured out the A button. Tap it, and Ecco sings — a sonar pulse that ripples
  outward through the water in every direction. Hold it, and the song comes back with information: a
  map of the surrounding passages, a picture of what's ahead, a signal from the creatures willing to
  help you versus the ones just waiting to end your run.

[H3]  [5w]
  The ocean didn't get smaller.

[BODY]  [43w]
  You just stopped reacting to all of it. That one button changed everything. Not because it made
  the game easier — Ecco never got easy — but because it gave you a way to separate noise from
  signal before you committed to moving.

[H3]  [9w]
  Attention Is the Ocean. Intention Is the Ping Back.

[BODY]  [35w]
  Your campaigns are generating attention. The reach numbers look good. Leadership is nodding. And
  then the sales team asks what happened to the pipeline, and everyone goes quiet. Attention is
  noise. Intention is a signal.

[BODY]  [50w]
  Most marketing teams we've worked with spend the better part of their budget broadcasting into the
  ocean — impressions, reach, share of voice — and call it strategy. What they're missing is the
  pause after the pulse. The moment where you hold A and actually listen to what comes back.

[BODY]  [53w]
  1992. A lone dolphin. An ocean full of things that wanted to eat you. Somewhere along the way, you
  learned to stop chasing and start listening. One sonar pulse, and suddenly the noise had a shape.
  Reflecting on it nearly twenty-five years later, we find a marketing lesson inside this simple
  game mechanic.

[H3]  [8w]
  Most Teams Never Hold the Button Long Enough.

[BODY]  [65w]
  Here's the discipline that separates good marketing from expensive noise: emit something specific
  enough that only the right people respond, then measure the response — not the emission. Not who
  saw the ad. Who came back to pricing after seeing it? Not who followed. Who downloaded the thing
  only someone close to a decision would bother with? Not who opened the email. Who hit reply?

[PULL QUOTE]  [14w]
  "Those are the creatures worth swimming toward. Everything else is still just open water."

[H3]  [5w]
  The signal is always there.

[BODY]  [33w]
  Ecco's sonar didn't create new information. The passages were always hidden. The allies were
  already in the water. The map existed. He just had to stop charging forward long enough to hear
  it.

[BODY]  [26w]
  Your market is the same. The buyers with real intentions are already in your ocean. They're
  pinging back. Most brands are too busy broadcasting to notice.
